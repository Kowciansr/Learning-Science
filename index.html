Learning Science and Theory

Every design decision you make either works with the way the brain learns or against it. This lesson shows you the science behind those decisions, and how to turn it into practical design choices.

45 to 60 min · self-paced 4 objectives · Bloom's aligned No prerequisites
What you will be able to do
  • Explain how memory, attention, and cognitive load shape learning (LO1)
  • Compare behaviorism, cognitivism, and constructivism as explanations of how learning happens (LO2)
  • Identify evidence-based strategies (retrieval, spacing, interleaving, feedback) in course designs (LO3)
  • Select a theory-informed design approach for a given learning scenario (LO4)

Section 1

Why Learning Science Matters

Picture two versions of the same compliance course. Version A presents all the content, then ends with a quiz. Version B mixes short practice questions into the content, spreads key ideas across the course, and asks learners to recall information before re-teaching it. Same content in both. Six weeks later, the Version B learners remember far more.

That gap is not luck. It is learning science at work: the study of how people learn, built from cognitive psychology, neuroscience, and education research. And you just felt why you need it. If any of the myth cards above surprised you, your instincts and the evidence disagree.

Key point

Learning science lets you decide with evidence instead of instinct. When the two disagree, go with the evidence.

What "learning" actually is

Before you can design for learning, you need a working definition. Here is the one this lesson uses: a relatively permanent change in knowledge or behavior that results from experience. Two words in that sentence carry most of the weight.

Permanent. If a learner passes the quiz today but cannot apply the skill next month, durable learning never happened. You measured a moment, not a change.

Change. Learning has to show up somewhere you can see it, in what a person knows or does. That is why measurable learning objectives sit at the center of instructional design. No visible change, no evidence of learning.

Section 2 LO1 · Understand

How the Brain Learns

You now have a definition of learning. The next question is what has to happen inside a learner's head for that change to stick, and where the process breaks down.

The memory model every designer should know

Think of memory as three stores in a row. Tap each store in the diagram to see what it does to your designs.

Fig. 1 · The three-store (modal) model of memory, after Atkinson and Shiffrin (1968). Every piece of learning has to squeeze through the working memory bottleneck.

Example: the 30-step demo

A learner watches a 12-minute software demo containing 30 distinct steps. Working memory cannot hold 30 new steps. Most are gone before they can be encoded. Now redesign it: three 4-minute segments, each organized around one meaningful task, each followed by hands-on practice. Each segment now fits inside working memory, and the practice does the encoding.

Cognitive Load Theory: run the lab

If working memory is the bottleneck, the obvious next question is what fills it up. Cognitive Load Theory, developed by John Sweller, gives you three answers. Intrinsic load comes from the complexity of the material itself. Extraneous load is effort wasted on things that teach nothing. Germane load is the productive effort of building schemas.

Reading about load is one thing. Watch it happen. Below is a working memory meter for a lesson on a genuinely complex topic. Toggle the design decisions and see what fills the learner's head.

Intrinsic (manage it) Extraneous (cut it) Germane (feed it) Capacity = 100%

The percentages are a teaching model, not measured units: cognitive load is a construct, not a gauge. And a note for the theory-minded: current CLT treats germane load as working-memory resources devoted to the intrinsic load of the task rather than a third additive load (Sweller, 2010). The design implication is unchanged. Manage, cut, feed.

Key point

Manage intrinsic load. Cut extraneous load. Feed germane load. That is the designer's core cognitive job in one line.

Apply it in your own work

Open a course or tutorial you worked on recently. Find one element that adds extraneous load, something learners must process that contributes nothing to the objective. What would you cut or simplify? Name the load category your fix addresses.

Section 3 LO2 · Analyze

The Big Three Learning Theories

You can now describe the machinery of memory. But machinery alone does not tell you what learning is or how to cause it. That is the job of learning theories. Three of them have shaped nearly every learning product you have ever used, and none of them is simply "correct." Each explains part of the picture. Expert designers move among them on purpose.

Learning as observable behavior change

Core idea: Learning is a change in observable behavior, shaped by stimulus, response, and consequence. Reinforcement strengthens behavior. What happens inside the mind stays a black box.

Key figuresPavlov · classical conditioningSkinner · operant conditioningThorndike · law of effect

What it looks like in EdTech:

  • Immediate feedback on quiz answers
  • Points, badges, streaks, and other gamification mechanics
  • Drill-and-practice apps such as flashcards and typing tutors
  • Mastery gates: demonstrate the behavior before advancing

Best suited for: procedural skills, fluency building, compliance behaviors, habit formation.

Limitation: It explains what learners do, not how they think. Ask it to handle complex reasoning or transfer and it goes quiet.

Learning as information processing

Behaviorism refused to look inside the head. Cognitivism opened the box.

Core idea: The mind is an active information-processing system. Learning means taking in information, organizing it, connecting it to prior knowledge, and storing it in long-term memory. Everything in Section 2 is cognitivist science.

Key figuresAtkinson & Shiffrin · modal modelMiller · memory spanSweller · cognitive loadMayer · multimedia learning

What it looks like in EdTech:

  • Chunked microlearning sized to working memory limits
  • Advance organizers, concept maps, clear content hierarchies
  • Worked examples that fade into independent practice
  • Multimedia pairing narration with relevant visuals (Mayer's principles)

Best suited for: conceptual knowledge, structured skill-building, any content where complexity is the enemy.

Limitation: It can treat learning as a solo, context-free process, and quietly ignore motivation, social interaction, and authentic application.

Learning as meaning-making

Cognitivism explains how information gets stored. It says less about where understanding comes from. Constructivism picks up that thread.

Core idea: Learners do not receive knowledge. They build it, by connecting new experiences to what they already know. Social constructivism (Vygotsky) adds that this building happens through interaction with others, and that instruction works best when aimed at the Zone of Proximal Development: the space between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with support. That support has a name you already use, coined by Wood, Bruner, and Ross in 1976. Scaffolding.

Key figuresPiaget · cognitive constructivismVygotsky · social constructivism, ZPDBruner · discovery learning, scaffoldingDewey · learning by doing

What it looks like in EdTech:

  • Scenario-based and problem-based learning
  • Simulations, virtual labs, project-based capstones
  • Discussion forums, peer review, cohort-based courses
  • Scaffolds (hints, templates, coaching) that fade as competence grows

Best suited for: complex judgment, messy ill-structured problems, transfer to the real world.

Limitation: Pure discovery learning can overwhelm a novice. When learners have no prior knowledge to build on, unguided construction is slow and painful.

Key point

Pick the theory that fits the learning problem, not the one that is fashionable. Fluency drills call for behaviorist design. Complex judgment calls for constructivist design. Both lean on cognitivist principles to avoid overload.

Section 4 LO3 · Apply

From Theory to Practice: Evidence-Based Strategies

Theories tell you how learning works. They do not hand you a build spec. For that, turn to the strategies that decades of research keep confirming. Four of them belong in every designer's toolkit. Flip each card to see how it shows up in real course design.

See spacing work

The same amount of study spread across days beats one long session, and the reason surprises people: the small amount of forgetting between sessions is the point. Retrieving something you have partly forgotten is exactly the effort that makes memory stick. Toggle the two study plans below and watch what each one leaves behind after a month.

Fig. 2 · Schematic forgetting curves in the tradition of Ebbinghaus (1885). The shapes are illustrative, not plotted data. Each review interrupts forgetting, and the decay after each retrieval gets shallower.

Key point

Notice what these four strategies share. Each one feels harder to learners in the moment. Robert Bjork named these desirable difficulties. Design for durable learning, not for the illusion of ease.

Apply it in your own work

Audit one assessment you have built against all four strategies. Does the course test before the end? Do concepts come back over time? Are question types mixed? Does every option carry a rationale? Pick the single change with the highest impact.

Section 5 LO4 · Apply

Choosing Your Approach: Theory in Action

You now hold theories in one hand and strategies in the other. The last skill is choosing, and there is no universal best method. Expert designers diagnose the learning problem first and select the approach second. So practice the diagnosis. Below are three client briefs. For each one, name the outcome type, then name the learners. Get both right and the design direction follows.

Step 2 · Diagnose the learners

Whichever brief you just diagnosed, step 3 never changes: layer on the evidence-based strategies. Retrieval, spacing, interleaving, and quality feedback apply to every approach.

Worked scenario: the hospital brief, diagnosed step by step

Brief: A hospital needs nurses to master a new medication-verification procedure. Errors are dangerous. The steps are fixed. The nurses range from new graduates to 20-year veterans.

Diagnosis: Walk the lens. The outcome is procedural accuracy under pressure, so the backbone is behaviorist: repeated practice to mastery with immediate corrective feedback. Intrinsic load will overwhelm the new graduates, so add cognitivist supports: chunk the procedure and open with a worked-example demonstration. The skill has to survive a chaotic real ward, so close with a constructivist capstone: a simulated shift with distractions, followed by a debrief. Retention has to last months, so schedule booster simulations at two and six weeks.

One design, three theories, each doing the job it does best.

Apply it · your turn to diagnose

A client asks for "a one-hour video course" to train customer-support reps to handle angry customers. Run the three-step lens: diagnose the outcome type, the likely learner range, and sketch a design that beats the one-hour video. Name the theory behind each design choice.

Summary

In this lesson, you defined learning as a relatively permanent change in knowledge or behavior, and traced the path that change must travel: through a severely limited working memory into long-term memory. Cognitive Load Theory gave you three levers to pull along that path. Manage intrinsic load, cut extraneous load, feed germane load. You compared the three major learning theories and saw that behaviorism, cognitivism, and constructivism each fit different learning problems rather than competing for a single crown. You then connected theory to four evidence-based strategies and practiced a three-step decision lens for matching theory-informed designs to real scenarios.

From here, every design review you sit in is a chance to ask the same quiet question: is this decision working with the brain, or against it?

Designer's notes and sources

Delivery and adaptation guide
  • Modularity: Sections 2 through 5 stand alone as 10-to-15-minute microlessons for drip delivery, which is itself spaced practice in action.
  • Practice the message: This lesson models its own principles: chunked sections, retrieval before summaries, cumulative checks, rationale-rich feedback. Point that out to learners. Meta-awareness aids transfer.
  • Live delivery: Convert the Apply It prompts into breakout activities. Use the hospital scenario as a small-group case before revealing the model answer.
  • Extension topics: andragogy (Knowles), motivation (self-determination theory, ARCS), Mayer's multimedia principles (15 in the current edition), connectivism and social learning in the age of AI, and measurement (Kirkpatrick, learning analytics).
